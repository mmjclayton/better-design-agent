## Evidence-Based Colour Associations

Colour responses are context-dependent and culturally moderated. These are Western/digital conventions, not universal truths:

| Colour | Common UI Usage | Evidence Level |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Red | Errors, destructive actions, urgency | Strong - physiological arousal response |
| Green | Success, confirmation, positive status | Moderate - cultural association |
| Blue | Links, primary actions, trust | Moderate - most consistently "preferred" colour |
| Yellow/Amber | Warnings, caution | Moderate - high visibility, attention-grabbing |
| Grey | Disabled states, secondary content | Strong - low contrast = low priority |

## Key Research Findings

1. **Context overrides association** - A red button labelled "Subscribe" is fine. Red means danger only when paired with danger semantics.

2. **Saturation affects perception** - High saturation = energy, urgency. Low saturation = calm, sophistication. This is more reliable than hue associations.

3. **Colour contrast improves readability by up to 40%** - The contrast ratio matters more than the specific colours chosen.

4. **Do not rely on colour alone** - WCAG 1.4.1 requires that colour is not the sole means of conveying information. Always pair colour with text, icons, or patterns.

## When to apply
- Semantic colour assignment: success/error/warning/info states
- CTA button colour selection: contrast and prominence matter more than hue
- Status indicators: always pair with text or icons
- Brand colour application: ensure WCAG contrast compliance
